Understand.com

Goals

Understand sells 3D medical animations on a subscription basis to customers around the world. These customers embed the Understand player into their websites via iFrame for patients to view and become better educated about the medical procedures offered. Currently, only 22% of users interact with the player and most of this interaction consists of simply playing and pausing the video they are watching. The Understand Player was redesigned with 2 main goals in mind: give it a more modern look and feel and increase user interaction.

Design

Previously, the animation menu was heavily text based with videos hidden behind menu catagories. The new player exposes videos more clearly and draws attention with graphics and large headings, as well as details about video views and likes. With a clear tab menu design there is now more space for videos, ideally encouraging users to explore animation categories and videos more. The second main improvement was removing unused features and redesigning the most used features to be more accesible and user friendly. Features such as the ability to share and like videos have come to be expected in modern media players and were therefore added to the new player.

Development

After the design was complete, I created a fully functional prototype with HTML, CSS and JavaScript. Usually after this step, I hand the designs and prototype to the front-end developers and aid them with any questions they may have. For the first time, I was given the opportunity to test my React skills and really put them to use. While the main structure and data gathering for the app was done by the development lead, I made all of the components for both the desktop and mobile versions of the menu and player. This was not only such a great learning experience, but I also felt so proud to take my designs farther than I ever had before.

Challenges

One major challenge was redesigning the seekbar to include both steps and doctors notes. Each video is broken up into logical chunks called steps which were previously displayed as a list to the side of each playing video. Analytics showed that only 1.5% of viewers actually clicked on these steps. In the redesign, I displayed the steps as notches in the seekbar as a way to save space as well as give them a contextually logical location. When users hover over the notches, tooltips appears displaying relevant step information and the ability to seek to this part of the video. Doctors also have the opportunity to attach a doctor's note to each step. This is a legacy feature that needed to be supported, despite its small current utility. I designed the notes as large popovers that appear above the current step notch as the video enters each step. One note is shown at a time and is shown until the user takes an action close it. Current use trends show that users rarely re-open the doctor's notes after actively closing them, therefore in the redesign, after actively closing the note the user has to rewind the video and enter the step again to view the note again. This decsion was made in an attempt to save space and increase usability. The doctor's notes did not become less of a challenge after the design was complete. Implementing these notes in React also proved to be a new and interesting challenge. Since these doctor's notes are fairly large and are placed based on the location of each step, some of the notes bled outside of the visible part of the player. To determine when to restyle these overflowing notes, I had to calculate each notes location and size and whether or not it was in the viewport.
